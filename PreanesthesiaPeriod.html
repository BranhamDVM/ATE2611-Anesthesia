1a. Identifying Preanesthetic Exam Equipment +

A thorough physical exam requires specific tools. Know them!

  • **Stethoscope:** For auscultation of the heart and lungs.
  • **Thermometer:** To accurately assess core body temperature (often rectal).
  • **Otoscope/Ophthalmoscope:** For detailed exam of ears/eyes (when indicated).
  • **Reflex Hammer:** To check neurological reflexes.
  • **Scale:** To obtain an accurate and current **patient weight** (crucial for drug dosing!).
  • **Watch/Timer with Second Hand:** For accurate measurement of heart rate, respiratory rate, and pulse quality.

1c & 1d. Regional Exam & Differentiating Normal/Abnormal +

We use a systematic, regional approach to ensure nothing is missed. **Focus on areas critical for anesthetic safety!**

**Cardiovascular System (CVS) Assessment**

  • **Normal:** Pink mucous membranes (MM), Capillary Refill Time (CRT) $<2$ seconds, normal heart rate (HR) and rhythm.
  • **Abnormal (Anesthetic Concerns):**
    • **Prolonged CRT ($>2$ sec) or Pale/Tacky MM:** Suggests poor perfusion, hypotension, or anemia.
    • **Murmurs:** Indicate valvular disease; may require cardiac workup (e.g., echocardiogram) and tailored anesthetic protocols.
    • **Arrhythmias:** Irregular heart rhythm; requires ECG evaluation.

**Respiratory System Assessment**

  • **Normal:** Eupnea (normal breathing), clear auscultation of all lung fields.
  • **Abnormal (Anesthetic Concerns):**
    • **Dyspnea (difficulty breathing) or Tachypnea (rapid breathing):** May indicate underlying pulmonary disease, requiring pre-oxygenation.
    • **Crackles/Wheezes:** Indicate fluid or obstruction; increases risk for hypoxemia under anesthesia.

üéØ Objective 2: Anesthetic Patient Classification (ASA)

2b. Applying the Classification System for Anesthetic Patients +

The **American Society of Anesthesiologists (ASA) Physical Status Classification System** is essential for estimating the anesthetic risk of a patient. You must assign a status (I-V, plus E) to every patient.

ASA Status Description Anesthetic Risk
**I** Normal, healthy patient (e.g., routine spay/neuter). Minimal
**II** Patient with mild systemic disease (e.g., controlled diabetes, well-controlled epilepsy, geriatric patient). Low
**III** Patient with severe systemic disease (e.g., moderate dehydration, stable heart disease, anemia). Moderate
**IV** Patient with severe systemic disease that is a constant threat to life (e.g., severe hemorrhage, uncompensated heart failure, renal failure). High
**V** Moribund patient who is not expected to survive without the operation (e.g., severe head trauma, gastric dilation-volvulus (GDV) shock). Extreme
**E** Added to any class (I-V) for **Emergency** procedures. Increased
2c. How ASA Status Affects Anesthetic Technique +

The higher the ASA status, the greater the risk, and the more tailored the protocol must be.

  • **ASA I/II:** These patients can generally tolerate standard anesthetic protocols. Monitoring is still vital, but the risk of major complications is lower.
  • **ASA III:** Requires **pre-anesthetic diagnostics** (blood work, radiographs) and **stabilization** (IV fluids, anti-arrhythmics) before anesthesia. The protocol will often involve lower doses and drugs with minimal cardiovascular depression.
  • **ASA IV/V:** These are our **highest-risk** patients. Anesthetic plans must be **multimodal** and often involve **injectable-only protocols** (Total Intravenous Anesthesia or TIVA) to better control depth. They require **aggressive pre-anesthetic stabilization**, extensive monitoring (e.g., direct blood pressure), and the availability of emergency drugs.
  • **The Technician's Role:** For high-risk patients (III, IV, V), your responsibility in **advanced monitoring** and **anticipating complications** increases dramatically.
