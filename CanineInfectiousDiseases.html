Canine Infectious Diseases

A Vet Tech's Comprehensive Study Guide

Canine Distemper Virus (CDV) >

Pathogen

A highly contagious, single-stranded RNA virus from the Morbillivirus genus, family Paramyxoviridae. It is related to measles in humans and rinderpest in cattle.

Transmission

Primarily transmitted via **aerosol droplets** from coughing or sneezing. The virus is also shed in all body secretions (ocular/nasal discharge, urine, feces) and is highly contagious, though it does not survive long in the environment.

Clinical Signs

CDV is a systemic disease, often presenting in stages. The initial stage often includes fever, anorexia, and leukopenia.

  • **Respiratory:bOculonasal discharge (often mucopurulent), coughing, dyspnea, and secondary bacterial pneumonia.
  • **Gastrointestinal:nVomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain.
  • **Neurological (Late Stage): Ataxia, paresis, seizures (classically "gum-chewing"), muscle twitching (myoclonus), nystagmus, and hyperesthesia.
  • **Dermal/Dental (Chronic/Recovery): Hyperkeratosis of the paw pads ("hard pad disease") and nasal planum, and enamel hypoplasia in young dogs.

Diagnostics

Diagnosis is based on clinical signs, vaccination history, and specific tests:

  • PCR: Used to detect viral nucleic acid in blood, urine, or conjunctival swabs (often preferred).
  • Immunohistochemistry/Fluorescent Antibody (FA) Test: Detects viral antigen in epithelial cells (e.g., conjunctival or footpad scrapings).
  • Serology: Paired serum titers to detect rising antibody levels (IgM/IgG).

Treatment

Treatment is largely supportive, focusing on managing symptoms and preventing secondary infections.

  • Fluid Therapy: To correct dehydration from GI losses.
  • Antibiotics: To treat or prevent secondary bacterial infections (especially pneumonia).
  • Anticonvulsants:bTo control seizures (e.g., phenobarbital, potassium bromide).
  • Nursing Care: Excellent hygiene and nutritional support are critical.

Prevention

Strict isolation of infected dogs. Vaccination is the cornerstone of prevention.

Prognosis

Guarded to Poor, especially once neurological signs appear. High mortality rate (up to 50% in adults and 80% in puppies). Survivors often have permanent neurological deficits (e.g., myoclonus).

Vaccination Schedule

Included in the core combination vaccine **(DA2PP or DHPP)**.

  1. Puppies: Starting at 6-8 weeks of age, then every 3-4 weeks until 16 weeks of age.
  2. Adults: Booster one year after the final puppy shot, then typically every 3 years thereafter, based on risk assessment (often combined with Parvovirus, Adenovirus, etc.).
Canine Parvovirus Type 2 (CPV-2) >

Pathogen

A highly resilient, non-enveloped, single-stranded **DNA virus** from the *Parvoviridae* family.

Transmission

Extremely high concentrations of virus are shed in the **feces** of infected dogs. Transmission is primarily **fecal-oral**, but also through contaminated fomites (shoes, clothes, food bowls, floors).

**Environmental Resilience:** CPV is extremely stable and can survive for months or even years in the environment, especially in dirt. This makes environmental **disinfection** critical.

Clinical Signs

The virus targets rapidly dividing cells, especially those in the intestinal lining and bone marrow.

  • **Severe Gastroenteritis:** Profuse, watery, often bloody diarrhea, severe vomiting.
  • **Systemic:** Anorexia, depression, high fever, and rapid dehydration.
  • **Hematological:** **Severe leukopenia** (low white blood cell count) and neutropenia due to bone marrow suppression, leading to immunosuppression and high risk of septicemia.

Diagnostics

  • **Fecal ELISA Snap Test:** Rapid in-clinic test detecting CPV antigen in feces. False negatives can occur early in the disease or after viral shedding decreases.
  • **CBC:** Reveals characteristic **leukopenia** and neutropenia.
  • **PCR:** Highly sensitive test confirming the presence of the virus.

Treatment

Intensive **supportive care** is mandatory and often requires hospitalization for 3-7 days.

  • **Aggressive IV Fluid Therapy:** To combat severe dehydration and electrolyte imbalances.
  • **Broad-Spectrum Antibiotics:** Essential to prevent/treat **septicemia** (secondary bacterial translocation from the damaged gut into the bloodstream).
  • **Antiemetics & Pain Management:** To control vomiting and abdominal discomfort.
  • **Nutritional Support:** Feeding should be resumed as soon as vomiting is controlled.

Prognosis

**Guarded**, particularly for young puppies. With intensive veterinary care, the survival rate is high (around 80-90%). Without treatment, the prognosis is very poor.

Vaccination Schedule

Core vaccine component **(DA2PP or DHPP)**.

  1. **Puppies:** Starting at 6-8 weeks of age, then every 3-4 weeks until **16-20 weeks of age** (to overcome maternal antibody interference, which is a major concern).
  2. **Adults:** Booster one year after the final puppy shot, then typically every 3 years.
Canine Infectious Hepatitis (ICH) - Adenovirus Type 1 >

Pathogen

Caused by **Canine Adenovirus 1 (CAV-1)**, a non-enveloped DNA virus.

Transmission

Transmitted via contact with the **urine, feces, or saliva** of infected dogs. The virus localizes in the kidney and is shed in the urine for months after clinical recovery.

Clinical Signs

The virus primarily targets the liver, kidneys, and endothelial cells (blood vessel lining).

  • **Acute Form (Puppies):** High fever, lethargy, anorexia, vomiting, bloody diarrhea, and potentially death.
  • **Classic Form:** Fever, mucosal petechiae/ecchymoses (due to endothelial damage and coagulopathy), tonsillitis, and abdominal pain.
  • **Ocular Signs:** The characteristic **"blue eye"** or corneal edema (due to immune complex deposition) often develops 7-10 days after acute illness.

Diagnostics

  • **Coagulation Tests:** Prolonged clotting times (indicative of liver failure).
  • **Chemistry Panel:** Elevated liver enzymes (ALT, ALP), bilirubin.
  • **PCR/Virus Isolation:** Detection in urine, feces, or tissue samples.
  • **Necropsy:** Intranuclear inclusion bodies in liver cells.

Treatment

**Supportive care** and aggressive treatment of hemorrhage and coagulopathies.

  • **IV Fluids and Electrolyte Management:** To maintain hydration.
  • **Blood Transfusions:** May be required for severe hemorrhage or coagulopathy.
  • **Nutritional Support:** High-quality, easily digestible diet.

Prevention

The vaccine is a core component **(DA2PP or DHPP)**. It uses **Canine Adenovirus 2 (CAV-2)**, which is closely related to CAV-1 but does not cause the "blue eye" side effect.

Prognosis

**Fair** for the mild form, but **guarded to poor** for the peracute or severe form, especially in puppies.

Vaccination Schedule

Core vaccine component **(DA2PP or DHPP)**, using **CAV-2** antigen.

  1. **Puppies:** Starting at 6-8 weeks of age, then every 3-4 weeks until 16 weeks of age.
  2. **Adults:** Booster one year after the final puppy shot, then typically every 3 years.
Infectious Tracheobronchitis (Kennel Cough) >

Pathogen

A syndrome caused by a combination of agents, most commonly **Bordetella bronchiseptica** (bacterium) and **Canine Parainfluenza Virus (CPIV)** or **Canine Adenovirus 2 (CAV-2)** (viruses).

Transmission

Highly contagious. Transmitted via **aerosolized droplets** (coughing) in high-density environments (kennels, dog parks, shelters).

Clinical Signs

The primary symptom is a classic, dry, hacking **"honking" cough**, often exacerbated by exercise or excitement.

  • The cough is typically non-productive and sounds like the dog is trying to dislodge something from its throat.
  • Other signs include retching, gagging, and mild nasal discharge.
  • Most dogs remain alert, active, and afebrile (without fever).

Diagnostics

  • **Clinical Signs:** Usually diagnosed based on the classic cough and history of exposure.
  • **Tracheal Pinch:** Eliciting a forceful cough upon light palpation of the trachea.
  • **Culture/PCR:** Swabs of the oropharynx or trachea can confirm the causative agents (especially *Bordetella*).

Treatment

Often self-limiting (resolves in 1-3 weeks). Treatment focuses on symptom relief.

  • **Cough Suppressants:** To reduce the intensity and frequency of the cough (especially at night).
  • **Antibiotics (if needed):** Only prescribed if a secondary bacterial infection is suspected (e.g., *Bordetella*) or if the dog is systemically ill.

Prognosis

Excellent for most healthy dogs. The disease is usually mild and self-limiting.

Vaccination Schedule

Available as a **Non-Core** vaccine, highly recommended for dogs that board, attend daycare, or frequent dog parks.

  • **Intranasal:** Provides the fastest local immunity (within 72 hours). Given annually.
  • **Oral:** Provides mucosal immunity. Given annually.
  • **Injectable:** Slower onset of immunity. Given 2 doses, 2-4 weeks apart, then annually.
Leptospirosis >

Pathogen

Caused by spiral-shaped bacteria (**spirochetes**) of the genus *Leptospira*. It has many serovars (strains), such as *L. interrogans*.

Transmission

Transmission occurs through contact with **contaminated water, soil, or urine** from infected wild animals (e.g., rodents, raccoons, deer). The spirochete enters the body through mucous membranes or abraded skin.

**CRITICAL Zoonotic Risk:** Leptospirosis is a serious zoonotic disease. Vet Techs must use **strict hygiene, wear gloves, and handle contaminated urine with extreme caution.**

Clinical Signs

Targets the kidneys and liver.

  • **Acute Kidney Injury (AKI):** Oliguria/anuria, polyuria/polydipsia, vomiting, painful abdomen.
  • **Hepatopathy (Liver Disease):** Jaundice (icterus), vomiting, anorexia.
  • **Other:** Fever, uveitis, muscle tenderness/shivering, and reluctance to move.

Diagnostics

  • **Microscopic Agglutination Test (MAT):** Gold standard for serology, detects antibodies. Requires paired samples.
  • **PCR:** Detects the *Leptospira* DNA in blood (early) and urine (after 7-10 days).
  • **Chemistry Panel/Urinalysis:** Azotemia, elevated creatinine/BUN, elevated liver enzymes, and presence of renal casts in the urine.

Treatment

Immediate and aggressive therapy is vital.

  • **Antibiotics:** **Penicillins** (e.g., Amoxicillin/Ampicillin) are used initially to clear the bloodstream, followed by **Doxycycline** to eliminate the carrier state (clear the bacteria from the kidneys).
  • **Supportive Care:** IV fluids to manage kidney failure, antiemetics, and strict output monitoring.

Prognosis

**Guarded to Fair**. Prognosis depends heavily on the severity of kidney and liver damage. Early and aggressive treatment improves the outcome significantly.

Vaccination Schedule

Available as a **Non-Core** vaccine, highly recommended for dogs in endemic areas or those with outdoor exposure.

  1. **Initial:** Two doses, 2-4 weeks apart.
  2. **Booster:** Annual vaccination is required for continued protection.
Canine Influenza Virus (CIV) - H3N8 & H3N2 >

Pathogen

Two distinct **RNA viruses** from the *Orthomyxoviridae* family: **H3N8** (equine origin) and **H3N2** (avian origin).

Transmission

Highly contagious. Transmitted via **aerosol droplets** (coughing/sneezing) and contaminated objects (fomites). Virtually 100% of exposed, naive dogs will become infected.

Clinical Signs

Signs mimic "kennel cough" but are generally more severe and often associated with fever.

  • **Mild Form:** Soft, moist cough, fever, serous nasal discharge that may become mucopurulent with secondary infection.
  • **Severe Form:** High fever ($$104^\circ$$F to $$106^\circ$$F), signs of severe hemorrhagic pneumonia, dyspnea, and rapid progression to death.

Diagnostics

  • **PCR:** Detects viral RNA in nasal/pharyngeal swabs (most useful early in the course of the disease).
  • **Serology:** Paired serum samples to show a four-fold increase in antibody titer (useful for confirmation after recovery).

Treatment

Primarily **supportive** care, similar to kennel cough.

  • **Fluids and Nutrition:** To maintain hydration and strength.
  • **Antibiotics:** Only used if secondary bacterial pneumonia is suspected or confirmed.
  • **Isolation:** Strict isolation for 2-4 weeks is required to prevent spread.

Prognosis

**Excellent** for the mild form ($$<10\%$$ mortality). The prognosis is **guarded** for the severe, hemorrhagic pneumonia form.

Prevention/Vaccination

Available as a **Non-Core** vaccine, recommended for dogs with high-risk exposure.

  1. **Vaccine Type:** Combined vaccine protecting against both **H3N8** and **H3N2** is preferred.
  2. **Initial:** Two doses, 2-4 weeks apart.
  3. **Booster:** Annual vaccination thereafter.
